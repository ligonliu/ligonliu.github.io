On fairness of wireless channel allocation/contention schemes

what does fairness mean in allocating channel to interfering nodes On fairness of a channel allocation or contention scheme

Single channel allocation

suppose the set of all nodes who need to use a single channel resource is V, their interference graph is (V,E), E={(i,j)|i, j ∈ V using the same channel interfere with each other}
  • each valid allocation is a Maximal Independent Set (MaxIS) of (V,E), denoted here by lower case letter s .
  • the set of all valid allocations of (V,E) to be denoted S = { s 1 , s 2 ... s m } .
  • a single channel allocation scheme has a probability mass function for a probability distribution on S : p 1 , p 2 , … , p m , ∑ i=1 m p i =1 , and ∀ i : p i ⩾ 0 is probability to choose the i th allocation.

Fairness Condition

the condition that a random channel allocation scheme is "fair":
∀ j ∈ V: ∑ i:j ∈ s i p i = 1 | V |
∑ i=1 m p i =1
We can see this is a linear system for { p 1 , p 2 , … , p m } with | V | +1 equations and m variables ( p 1 , p 2 , … , p m ). Depending on the interference graph, the linear system either:
  1. Has no non-negative solution of { p 1 , p 2 , … , p m } .
    This happens in two cases:
    (1) Has no solution of { p 1 , p 2 , … , p m }
    (2) Has real number solution of { p 1 , p 2 , … , p m } , but at least one of { p 1 , p 2 , … , p m } is negative.
  2. Has only one non-negative solution of { p 1 , p 2 , … , p m } .
  3. Has infinitely many solutions of { p 1 , p 2 , … , p m } .
A standard linear systems solver can easily decide which case applies from those 3, given the equations.

Fair allocations usually don't maximize throughput

The highest throughput is acquired by always allocating the channel to the largest MaxIS.
Thus, except uncommon cases like fully connected or null interference graph, or a graph with high symmetry among nodes, fair allocations don't maximize throughput.
