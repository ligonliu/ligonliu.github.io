Minimum water body area for water-source AC and heat pump

A few examples of minimum water body area for ACs and heat pumps that use a water body as heat/cool source. Water to water self-contained heat pump unit – advantages and example water body calculations

Introduction

Heat pump is a low carbon solution of heating and cooling in temperate and shore regions.  For brevity, we skip fundamentals of heat pumps. Readers unfamiliar with this topic can watch this video for the basics.

Here I point out how water-to-water self-contained heat pumps overcome some disadvantages of air-to-air heat pump that hampers its adoption.

Advantages of water to water

Compact, lightweight

Water-to-refrigerant heat exchangers are small and light. They can be made of corrosion resistant metal such as stainless steel.  Small and light translate to cost savings on shipping and installation.

Quiet

Compared to condenser fans, water pumps generate much lower audible noise.

Consumer installable

Split AC units have to be installed by certified HVAC technicians. Self-contained AC units are  installable by homeowners and unskilled personnel, reducing professional labor cost.

Water to water heat pump units convey heat or cool to the rooms with chill or hot circulation water. Compared to minisplits and central AC, this avoids routing refrigeration lines or air ducts between rooms.  Running low pressure circulation water pipes and finding leaks on them is a much easier task for consumers.

Propane as refrigerant

Propane(R290) is a cheap and ecofriendly flammable refrigerant with nice refrigeration properties. Propane is less than $2/kg, compared to R407c and R410a that costs $7-$20 per kg (typical residential systems hold between 2 and 10 kg of refrigerant)

It also works the best with the cheapest type of lubricant oil - mineral oil, unlike newer refrigerants that requires the more expensive POE oil.

Its usage in indoor installations are limited by fire risk concerns. Water-to-water heat pumps eliminate the risk by submerging the unit in water and limiting refrigerant outside buildings.

Hard to steal

Thieves don’t like to dive under water to steal an underwater packaged chiller/heater unit that hardly contains any precious metal. The refrigerant is neither worth stealing.

The water body

By putting the outdoor unit in water, we are actually using the water body as a giant heatsink.

For places with a natural water body, such as a pond, river, lake or sea, we don’t need much calculations, just put the unit deep enough to have contact with never freezing water.

For places without a natural water body, we need to create an artificial pond or pool, and fill it with a cheap water source, such as rainwater and graywater.  How to size the volume and surface area of this artificial pond?

Small water body for the summer

It is easier to have a small artificial pond for summer cooling than for winter heating. In summer, the surface area of pond will evaporate, taking out heat from condenser. During winter, the surface could freeze when air temperature is below freezing. While it’s easier for a heat pump to extract heat from liquid water, the extraction stops once the small water body freeze into an iceberg.

According to EngineeringToolbox,

Evaporation of water from a water surface

like an open tank, a swimming pool or similar - depends on water temperature, air temperature, air humidity and air velocity above the water surface.

The amount of evaporated water can be expressed as:

gs = ΘA(xs – x) / 3600  : amount of evaporated water per second (kg/s)

gh = ΘA(xs –x)  : amount of evaporated water per hour (kg/h)

where

Θ = (25+19v) : evaporation coefficient (kg/m2h)

v: velocity of air above the water surface (m/s)

A: water surface area (m2)

xs : maximum humidity ratio of saturated air at the same temperature as the water surface (kg/kg)  (kg H2O in kg Dry Air)

x: humidity ratio of air (kg/kg)  (kg H2O in kg Dry Air)

Required Heat Supply

Most of the heat or energy required for the evaporation is taken from the water itself. To maintain the water temperature, heat must be supplied to the water.

Required heat to cover evaporation can be calculated as

q = hwe gs

where

q = heat supplied (kW, 1 kW = 3412 Btu/h)

hwe = evaporation heat of water at temperature (kJ/kg)

Example: 45000 BTU/h AC water-cooled condenser

Heat Pumps

The aforementioned small water body obviously cannot be used as a heat pump’s heat source. Water’s fusion heat is 333J/g, 13 ton of water gives out 13000000*333 = 4.329e9 joule = 4.103e6 BTU (68 hours for the 60000 BTU/h heat pump) before it all freezes up into a giant block of ice. We cannot replenish water of a frozen pool.

However, anywhere adjacent to a large body of water, such as river or lake, have no problem pumping out heat from it. Because water’s density is maximized at 4°C, water colder than 4°C will convection up to the surface, where it could freeze into ice, rather than down. Thus, a well designed heat pump heat exchanger deep below surface will not freeze up on itself.
