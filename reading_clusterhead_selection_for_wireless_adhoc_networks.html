In wireless sensor networks, clustering is a common approach to simplify routing and reduce energy consumption. A temporary cluster head(CH) is usually generated in the cluster to serve tasks traditionally accomplished by base station or access point.

Scenarios and network structure

There are different scenarios where the sensor network is operated, resulting in different optimal strategies. Scenarios might happen that networks nodes are fixed or mobile, energy constraint or energy abundant, and the pattern of data flow are different among networks.

Moving node add to the complexity of clustering. The structure must react accordingly to the change of inter-node connectivity. Data might be continuously or intermittently generated from the networks. There may be nodes which aggregate continuous information from other node, do analysis and generate a batch transmission to be received by upper level.

The scenario that is earliest and most frequently studied is fixed sensor network, all nodes are with energy constraint, and data flow is “aggregation” based, where sensor nodes do not need sensed data among themselves instead send them to a command center in or outside the network. In this case, CH suffers from battery drain, because it has to use transmission power to route packets from all other nodes in the cluster. The solution is usually making all nodes in the cluster take turns to be the CH.

There can be mesh or hierarchical structures among CHs. In hierarchical structure, communication between two CHs is via an upper layer; In mesh structure, communication between two CHs are routed ad-hoc. [2]

Typical Algorithms

LEACH

LEACH[1] is an important traditional algorithm invented in year 2000. It was invented against the claim that “minimum transmission energy” (MTE) protocols made. Instead of their original claim for minimized the total transmission energy, the total energy consumption in multi-hopping message might even be higher than infrastructure network. It argues, if MTE routing is employed for power constrained device, the device closest to base station will exhaust its battery sooner and die early, resulting in uneven battery life of all the nodes, creating functional problem of the network.

In LEACH, nodes make autonomous decisions without any centralized control. Initially a node randomly decides to be or not to be a CH with probability p or (1-p). If the node decides to be CH, it broadcasts its decision. Each node deciding non-CH selects its cluster by choosing the CH closest to it measured by least transmission energy.

The role of CH is rotated periodically among the nodes. In each rotation phase, each node chooses a random number between 0 and 1 to become CH or not. Of course, the number of nodes deciding to be CH in each area or existing cluster follows Poisson distribution, so there will be fluctuates in configuration.

The problems with the original LEACH algorithm, for example, the probability of becoming a CH is fixed rather than decided considering link availability(to BS and other nodes) and battery status, which would occasionally drain battery. Also, the densities of CHs are not guaranteed to be evenly distributed.[2]

HEED

HEED (Hybrid Energy-Efficient Distributed clustering) was invented in 2004, addressing the need for rotating cluster role for battery power conservation.

HEED has three phases of execution[2]:

  1. 1.Initialization: 

Every node discovers their neighbors within the cluster range.

Every node compute its own initial CH probability CHprob = max(f(Er/Emax), p_min), where Er is residual energy, and Emax is maximum energy, p_min is the parameter (minimum probability) set for fast convergence.

  1. 2.Main processing: 

Repeat{

For any node v in the network,

If v received some CH messages, choose one head with minimum cost

If v does not have a CH, elect itself to become a CHwith CHprob.

CHprob  min(CHprob * 2, 1)

}

until CHprob = 1

  1. 3.Finalization: 

for each node:

If CH is found, join its cluster

Otherwise, elect itself to be CH

However, CHprob doubles in every step, and phase II of the protocol terminates one step (iteration) after CHprob reaches 1.

The HEED authors did some simulation on HEED and a modified version of LEACH, and find out that HEED has superior performance in aspects of Network lifetime, Ratio of energy used in clustering in total energy consumption and Network lifetime for a 500-node network as the sink travels farther.

 

Those three graphs are from the HEED paper. They find out HEED’s min degree variant and max degree variant has similar performance in network lifetime and energy consumption, while both is higher performance compared to the LEACH algorithm they are benchmarking with.

 

Comments

The LEACH and HEED are typical algorithms used in CH selection, independently without routing protocol. They solve the data aggregation problem in simple and decentralized manner, minimized message exchange between nodes and maintained computational cost as low as log or constant.

Further research could direct into modeling time sequence of data aggregation, and see if optimizing schedule of messages can provide even lower battery power consumption. Also, mesh-like multi-hop transmission can be investigated, as an alternative to agglomerative protocols.
